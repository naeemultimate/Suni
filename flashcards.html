Peak Expiratory Flow Rate (PEFR) Measurement

Definition: A measure of how fast a person can exhale, indicating airway obstruction.

Key Steps:

  • Ensure patient is upright & relaxed
  • Attach disposable mouthpiece
  • Take a deep breath in
  • Blow out as hard & fast as possible
  • Record highest of three readings
  • Compare to personal best or predicted values

Errors to Avoid:

  • ❌ Poor effort/incomplete breath
  • ❌ Tongue blocking mouthpiece
  • ❌ Coughing during test

Management:

If PEFR is low → Adjust medication or seek medical review

Knee Examination (MSK)

Definition: A systematic approach to examining the knee joint for pathology.

Key Steps:

  • Introduce yourself & confirm patient details
  • Expose both legs & position the patient comfortably
  • Inspect for swelling, deformity, scars, or muscle wasting
  • Palpate joint line, patella, & popliteal fossa for tenderness
  • Assess range of motion (flexion, extension)
  • Special tests: Lachman’s (ACL), McMurray’s (meniscus), Valgus/Varus stress (MCL/LCL)

Key Findings:

  • Joint effusion → Consider arthritis, trauma
  • Positive McMurray’s → Meniscal tear
  • Anterior translation on Lachman’s → ACL rupture

Cranial Nerve Examination

Definition: A neurological assessment of the 12 cranial nerves.

Key Steps:

  • Olfactory (I): Smell test
  • Optic (II): Visual acuity, fields, & pupillary reflex
  • Oculomotor, Trochlear, Abducens (III, IV, VI): Eye movements
  • Trigeminal (V): Facial sensation & jaw movements
  • Facial (VII): Facial expression & taste
  • Vestibulocochlear (VIII): Hearing & balance
  • Glossopharyngeal, Vagus (IX, X): Gag reflex, voice changes
  • Accessory (XI): Shoulder shrug
  • Hypoglossal (XII): Tongue movement

Key Findings:

  • Ptosis & pupil dilation → CN III palsy
  • Unilateral hearing loss → CN VIII dysfunction
  • Deviation of tongue → CN XII lesion

Thyroid Status Examination

Definition: A clinical examination to assess for signs of hypothyroidism or hyperthyroidism.

Key Steps:

  • General Inspection: Observe for weight changes, agitation, sweating, or dry skin.
  • Hands & Arms: Check for tremor (hyperthyroid), cold/dry skin (hypothyroid), palmar erythema, and pulse rate.
  • Face: Assess for periorbital swelling (hypothyroid), exophthalmos & lid lag (hyperthyroid).
  • Neck Examination:
    • Inspect for thyroid enlargement, scars, or asymmetry.
    • Palpate thyroid from behind, checking for size, consistency, nodules.
    • Auscultate for a thyroid bruit (suggests hyperthyroidism).
  • Special Tests:
    • Reflexes – brisk (hyperthyroid) or slow (hypothyroid).
    • Pretibial Myxoedema – associated with Graves' disease.
    • Proximal Muscle Weakness – test shoulder abduction against resistance.

Key Findings:

  • Hyperthyroid Signs: Tachycardia, weight loss, fine tremor, lid lag, exophthalmos.
  • Hypothyroid Signs: Bradycardia, weight gain, cold intolerance, dry skin, slow reflexes.
  • Goitre: Consider iodine deficiency, Graves' disease, or nodular thyroid disease.

Elbow Examination

Definition: A musculoskeletal assessment of the elbow joint to identify pathology such as arthritis, fractures, or ligament injuries.

Key Steps:

  • General Inspection: Check for swelling, erythema, deformity, scars, muscle wasting.
  • Palpation:
    • Assess for tenderness along the joint line, olecranon, medial and lateral epicondyles.
    • Check for effusion (boggy feel around the joint).
  • Range of Motion:
    • Flexion (C5/6) and Extension (C7).
    • Pronation and Supination (C6). Compare with the other arm.
  • Special Tests:
    • Resisted wrist extension – Lateral epicondylitis (Tennis elbow).
    • Resisted wrist flexion – Medial epicondylitis (Golfer’s elbow).
    • Tinel’s test – Ulnar nerve irritation at the cubital tunnel.

Key Findings:

  • Pain on resisted wrist extension → Tennis elbow (lateral epicondylitis).
  • Pain on resisted wrist flexion → Golfer’s elbow (medial epicondylitis).
  • Restricted movement → Arthritis, joint effusion.
  • Nerve involvement (ulnar, radial) → Weakness, sensory loss.

Upper Limb Neurological Examination

Definition: A neurological assessment of motor and sensory function in the upper limbs.

Key Steps:

  • General Inspection: Look for muscle wasting, fasciculations, tremors, scars.
  • Tone: Assess passive movement for hypertonia (spasticity, rigidity) or hypotonia.
  • Power: Test muscle strength for key movements:
    • Shoulder abduction and adduction
    • Elbow flexion and extension
    • Wrist extension and flexion
    • Finger extension, flexion, abduction
  • Reflexes: Test biceps (C5/6), triceps (C7), supinator (C6) reflexes.
  • Sensation: Assess light touch, pinprick, vibration and proprioception.
  • Coordination: Finger-to-nose test, rapid alternating movements.

Key Findings:

  • Hyperreflexia → Upper Motor Neuron (UMN) lesion
  • Hyporeflexia → Lower Motor Neuron (LMN) lesion
  • Weakness with fasciculations → LMN pathology
  • Intention tremor → Cerebellar disorder

Shoulder Examination

Definition: A musculoskeletal assessment of the shoulder joint to identify pathology such as rotator cuff injury, arthritis, or instability.

Key Steps:

  • General Inspection: Look for asymmetry, swelling, muscle wasting, scars, bruising.
  • Palpation:
    • Feel for tenderness at the sternoclavicular joint, acromioclavicular joint, and glenohumeral joint.
    • Palpate the rotator cuff tendons and long head of the biceps tendon.
  • Range of Motion:
    • Flexion and extension
    • Abduction and adduction
    • Internal and external rotation
  • Special Tests:
    • Neer’s test – Subacromial impingement.
    • Hawkins-Kennedy test – Subacromial impingement.
    • Empty Can test – Supraspinatus tear.
    • Lift-Off test – Subscapularis weakness.
    • Apprehension test – Shoulder instability.

Key Findings:

  • Reduced range of motion → Frozen shoulder or arthritis.
  • Positive Empty Can test → Supraspinatus pathology.
  • Positive Hawkins-Kennedy → Subacromial impingement.
  • Positive Apprehension test → Shoulder instability or dislocation history.

Hand Examination

Definition: A musculoskeletal assessment of the hands and wrists to identify pathology such as arthritis, nerve compression, or systemic disease manifestations.

Key Steps:

  • General Inspection: Look for joint swelling, deformities, muscle wasting, scars, or skin changes.
  • Palpation:
    • Feel for warmth, tenderness, and crepitus at the wrist, MCP, PIP, and DIP joints.
    • Assess for median, ulnar, and radial nerve compression (e.g., Tinel’s and Phalen’s test).
  • Range of Motion:
    • Flexion, extension, abduction, and adduction of fingers.
    • Thumb opposition and grip strength testing.
  • Special Tests:
    • Tinel’s and Phalen’s test – Carpal tunnel syndrome.
    • Froment’s sign – Ulnar nerve palsy.
    • Allens test – Vascular supply of the hand.

Key Findings:

  • Bouchard’s and Heberden’s nodes → Osteoarthritis.
  • Swan-neck and boutonniere deformities → Rheumatoid arthritis.
  • Thenar wasting → Median nerve pathology.
  • Ulnar claw → Ulnar nerve lesion.

Cardiovascular Examination

Definition: A systematic assessment of the heart and circulation to identify pathology such as heart failure, valve disease, or arrhythmias.

Key Steps:

  • General Inspection: Observe for cyanosis, clubbing, pallor, edema.
  • Hands & Arms: Assess capillary refill, splinter hemorrhages, Osler’s nodes, Janeway lesions.
  • Pulses:
    • Radial and brachial pulse – Rate, rhythm, volume.
    • Carotid pulse – Character and timing.
  • Neck Examination:
    • JVP assessment for raised venous pressure.
  • Chest Examination:
    • Palpate apex beat, heaves, and thrills.
    • Auscultate heart sounds (Aortic, Pulmonary, Tricuspid, Mitral areas).
  • Peripheral Signs: Check for pitting edema, sacral edema.

Key Findings:

  • Displaced apex beat → Left ventricular hypertrophy.
  • Raised JVP → Right heart failure.
  • Ejection systolic murmur → Aortic stenosis.
  • Pansystolic murmur → Mitral regurgitation.

Respiratory Examination

Definition: A systematic assessment of the respiratory system to identify conditions such as COPD, pneumonia, or pleural effusion.

Key Steps:

  • General Inspection: Observe for respiratory distress, accessory muscle use, central cyanosis.
  • Hands & Arms: Assess clubbing, cyanosis, asterixis (CO2 retention).
  • Neck Examination:
    • Check tracheal position – Deviation may indicate tension pneumothorax.
    • Assess for lymphadenopathy.
  • Chest Examination:
    • Palpation: Expansion symmetry, vocal fremitus.
    • Percussion: Hyper-resonant (pneumothorax), dull (effusion, consolidation).
    • Auscultation: Normal, wheeze, crackles, bronchial breathing.
  • Special Tests: Whispering pectoriloquy, tactile vocal fremitus.

Key Findings:

  • Hyper-resonance → Pneumothorax.
  • Dullness → Pleural effusion, pneumonia.
  • Wheeze → Asthma, COPD.
  • Coarse crackles → Pulmonary edema.

Abdominal Examination

Definition: A systematic assessment of the abdomen to identify gastrointestinal pathology.

Key Steps:

  • General Inspection: Look for scars, distension, visible peristalsis, caput medusae.
  • Hands & Arms: Check for clubbing, palmar erythema, asterixis, leukonychia.
  • Palpation:
    • Light and deep palpation of all four quadrants.
    • Assess for hepatomegaly, splenomegaly, and masses.
  • Percussion:
    • Check for shifting dullness (ascites).
    • Percuss liver and spleen size.
  • Auscultation: Listen for bowel sounds and bruits (aortic, renal, iliac).

Key Findings:

  • Absent bowel sounds → Paralytic ileus.
  • Tender hepatomegaly → Hepatitis, right heart failure.
  • Shifting dullness → Ascites.
  • Mass with pulsation → Aortic aneurysm.

Peripheral Vascular Examination

Definition: A clinical assessment of the arterial and venous circulation in the limbs.

Key Steps:

  • General Inspection: Look for skin color changes, ulcers, hair loss, swelling.
  • Palpation:
    • Assess temperature differences between limbs.
    • Check peripheral pulses (femoral, popliteal, dorsalis pedis, posterior tibial).
  • Capillary Refill: Assess perfusion by checking capillary refill time.
  • Special Tests:
    • Buerger’s test – Assess arterial sufficiency.
    • Trendelenburg test – Evaluate venous valve competence.

Key Findings:

  • Pale, cool limb → Arterial insufficiency.
  • Non-healing ulcer → Chronic venous insufficiency.
  • Unilateral swelling → Deep vein thrombosis.

Breast Examination

Definition: A clinical assessment of the breast to identify lumps, discharge, or skin changes.

Key Steps:

  • General Inspection: Observe for asymmetry, skin dimpling, nipple inversion, or discharge.
  • Palpation:
    • Systematic examination of all quadrants of the breast and axillary tail.
    • Assess for lumps, tenderness, or skin thickening.
  • Axillary Examination: Palpate for lymphadenopathy in axilla and supraclavicular region.

Key Findings:

  • Firm, immobile lump → Suggests malignancy.
  • Painful, mobile lump → Likely fibroadenoma or cyst.
  • Nipple retraction → Possible breast cancer.
  • Skin puckering → Suggestive of malignancy.

Obstetric Abdominal Examination

Definition: An assessment of the pregnant abdomen to evaluate fetal well-being and pregnancy progression.

Key Steps:

  • General Inspection: Look for size, scars, striae gravidarum, linea nigra.
  • Palpation:
    • Fundal height measurement (correlates with gestational age).
    • Assess fetal lie, presentation, and engagement.
  • Auscultation: Use Doppler or Pinard stethoscope to assess fetal heart rate.

Key Findings:

  • Fundal height discrepancy → Suspect growth restriction or polyhydramnios.
  • Non-cephalic presentation → Breech or transverse lie.
  • Reduced fetal movements → Requires urgent assessment.

Spine Examination

Definition: A musculoskeletal assessment of the spine to identify pathology such as scoliosis, kyphosis, or nerve root compression.

Key Steps:

  • General Inspection: Observe posture, spinal alignment, muscle wasting.
  • Palpation:
    • Assess for tenderness along the vertebral column.
    • Check for muscle spasm or step deformities.
  • Range of Motion:
    • Flexion, extension, lateral flexion, rotation.
  • Special Tests:
    • Schober’s test – Assess lumbar flexion (ankylosing spondylitis).
    • Straight leg raise – Sciatic nerve root irritation.

Key Findings:

  • Reduced lumbar flexion → Ankylosing spondylitis.
  • Asymmetry of shoulders or ribs → Scoliosis.
  • Pain with straight leg raise → Sciatica or lumbar disc herniation.

Hip Examination

Definition: A musculoskeletal assessment of the hip joint to identify pathology such as osteoarthritis, fractures, or soft tissue injuries.

Key Steps:

  • General Inspection: Look for leg length discrepancy, muscle wasting, scars, bruising.
  • Palpation:
    • Assess for tenderness at the hip joint and greater trochanter.
  • Range of Motion:
    • Flexion, extension, abduction, adduction, internal and external rotation.
  • Special Tests:
    • Trendelenburg test – Assess gluteus medius weakness.
    • Thomas test – Detect fixed flexion deformity.

Key Findings:

  • Reduced range of motion → Osteoarthritis.
  • Positive Trendelenburg test → Gluteus medius weakness.
  • Leg shortening → Hip fracture or dislocation.

Bimanual Vaginal Examination

Definition: An internal examination to assess the cervix, uterus, and adnexa for abnormalities.

Key Steps:

  • General Inspection: Check for external abnormalities, discharge, lesions.
  • Palpation:
    • Assess cervix position, consistency, and mobility.
    • Evaluate uterus size, tenderness, and mobility.
    • Palpate adnexa for masses or tenderness.

Key Findings:

  • Fixed retroverted uterus → Endometriosis.
  • Enlarged tender uterus → Pregnancy or fibroids.
  • Adnexal mass → Ovarian cyst or malignancy.

Speculum Examination

Definition: A gynaecological examination to visualise the cervix and vaginal walls.

Key Steps:

  • General Inspection: Look for lesions, discharge, bleeding.
  • Procedure:
    • Insert speculum at a slight downward angle.
    • Open blades and identify the cervix.
  • Assess:
    • Cervical appearance – Smooth, ulcerated, friable.
    • Presence of abnormal discharge or lesions.

Key Findings:

  • Ectropion → Normal physiological variant.
  • Friable cervix → Cervicitis, HPV changes.
  • White patches → Possible cervical intraepithelial neoplasia.

Testicular Examination

Definition: An examination of the scrotum and testes to identify lumps, swelling, or tenderness.

Key Steps:

  • General Inspection: Look for swelling, erythema, asymmetry.
  • Palpation:
    • Assess testicular size, consistency, and tenderness.
    • Examine epididymis and spermatic cord.
  • Special Tests:
    • Transillumination – Differentiate solid from cystic masses.
    • Cremasteric reflex – Assess neurological function.

Key Findings:

  • Painless lump → Testicular cancer suspicion.
  • Painful, swollen testis → Epididymo-orchitis.
  • Bag of worms sensation → Varicocele.

Rectal Examination

Definition: A digital rectal exam to assess for abnormalities in the prostate, rectum, or anal canal.

Key Steps:

  • General Inspection: Check for external abnormalities (hemorrhoids, fissures).
  • Digital Rectal Examination:
    • Assess prostate size, texture, tenderness.
    • Palpate for rectal masses or stool consistency.

Key Findings:

  • Enlarged, smooth prostate → Benign prostatic hyperplasia.
  • Hard, irregular prostate → Prostate cancer.
  • Mass in rectum → Tumor or fecal impaction.

Lower Limb Neurological Examination

Definition: An assessment of motor, sensory, and reflex function in the lower limbs.

Key Steps:

  • Power: Assess hip, knee, ankle, and toe movements.
  • Reflexes: Patellar (L3/4), Achilles (S1), plantar response.
  • Sensation: Test light touch, pinprick, vibration, and proprioception.
  • Coordination: Heel-to-shin test, gait assessment.

Key Findings:

  • Spasticity → Upper Motor Neuron lesion.
  • Hyporeflexia → Lower Motor Neuron lesion.

Ankle and Foot Examination

Definition: A musculoskeletal assessment of the ankle and foot to identify pathology such as arthritis, ligament injuries, or deformities.

Key Steps:

  • General Inspection: Look for swelling, erythema, deformities, scars, ulcers.
  • Palpation:
    • Assess for tenderness at the medial and lateral malleolus, Achilles tendon, and plantar fascia.
    • Check for temperature changes or effusion.
  • Range of Motion:
    • Dorsiflexion and plantarflexion.
    • Inversion and eversion.
    • Toe flexion and extension.
  • Special Tests:
    • Thompson test – Assess Achilles tendon rupture.
    • Anterior drawer test – Evaluate ankle instability.

Key Findings:

  • Pain on weight-bearing → Possible fracture or ligament injury.
  • Positive Thompson test → Achilles tendon rupture.
  • Restricted dorsiflexion → Achilles tendonitis or joint stiffness.

Diabetic Foot Examination

Definition: A systematic assessment of the feet in diabetic patients to identify complications such as neuropathy, ulcers, or vascular disease.

Key Steps:

  • General Inspection: Look for ulcers, deformities, calluses, infection, gangrene.
  • Palpation:
    • Assess for temperature changes, capillary refill time.
    • Check dorsalis pedis and posterior tibial pulses.
  • Neurological Testing:
    • Monofilament test – Assess for peripheral neuropathy.
    • Vibration test using a tuning fork.
    • Proprioception and light touch testing.
  • Special Tests:
    • Ankle Brachial Pressure Index (ABPI) – Assess arterial supply.

Key Findings:

  • Loss of sensation → Diabetic peripheral neuropathy.
  • Absent pulses → Peripheral arterial disease.
  • Foot ulcers → High risk of diabetic foot complications.

Mental State Examination

Definition: A structured assessment of a patient’s cognitive and emotional functioning.

Key Steps:

  • Appearance and Behaviour: Assess grooming, posture, eye contact, psychomotor activity.
  • Speech: Evaluate fluency, tone, rate, and volume.
  • Mood and Affect: Determine if mood is euthymic, depressed, or elevated.
  • Thought Process and Content:
    • Assess for delusions, paranoia, suicidal ideation.
  • Perception: Evaluate hallucinations (visual, auditory).
  • Cognition: Test orientation, memory, and attention.
  • Insight and Judgement: Assess the patient’s understanding of their condition.

Key Findings:

  • Flat affect → Depression.
  • Flight of ideas → Mania.
  • Auditory hallucinations → Schizophrenia.
  • Impaired orientation and memory → Dementia or delirium.
